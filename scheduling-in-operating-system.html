Process Scheduling and Schedulers in Operating Systems

Process Scheduling and Schedulers in Operating Systems

In multitasking operating systems, scheduling ensures efficient CPU usage by managing the order and execution of processes. Below is a breakdown of process queues, types of schedulers, and scheduling algorithms.

Types of Process Queues

Job Queue

  • Contains all the processes in the system.
  • Holds processes that are waiting for execution.

Ready Queue

  • Stores processes in main memory that are ready to run.
  • The CPU scheduler selects processes from this queue.
  • Typically implemented as a linked list.

Device Queue

  • Holds processes waiting for a particular I/O device.
  • Each device has its own queue.

Types of Schedulers

Long-Term Scheduler (Job Scheduler)

  • Selects processes from the job queue and loads them into memory.
  • Controls the degree of multiprogramming (number of processes in memory).
  • Aims to balance I/O-bound and CPU-bound processes.

Short-Term Scheduler (CPU Scheduler)

  • Selects processes from the ready queue for CPU execution.
  • Executes frequently to reduce CPU idle time.

Medium-Term Scheduler

  • Temporarily removes processes from memory (swapping).
  • Reduces the degree of multiprogramming.
  • Reintroduces processes when resources are available.

Importance of Scheduling

  • Maximizes resource utilization
  • Minimizes CPU idle time
  • Balances system load
  • Provides a smooth user experience

Process Scheduling

Need for Scheduling

  1. Efficient CPU utilization
  2. Handling multiple processes
  3. Avoiding deadlock and starvation
  4. Meeting deadlines in real-time systems

Factors Considered in Scheduling

  1. Process priority
  2. Arrival time
  3. Burst time
  4. I/O-bound vs CPU-bound
  5. Deadline requirements

Types of Scheduling

Preemptive Scheduling

  • CPU can be taken from a running process to serve a higher priority one.
  • Improves responsiveness but increases overhead.

Non-Preemptive Scheduling

  • Once a process starts, it runs until completion.
  • Simpler, but may cause long waiting times for short or high-priority processes.

CPU Scheduling Algorithms

First Come First Serve (FCFS)

  • Processes are served in the order they arrive.
  • Simple and fair, but can lead to high waiting time for short processes.

Shortest Job First (SJF)

  • Executes the process with the shortest burst time.
  • Minimizes average waiting time.
  • Can lead to starvation of longer processes.

Shortest Remaining Time First (SRTF)

  • Preemptive version of SJF.
  • Picks process with the least remaining execution time.
  • Lower turnaround time but increases overhead.

Highest Response Ratio Next (HRRN)

  • Response Ratio = (Waiting Time + Burst Time) / Burst Time
  • Balances waiting time and burst time.
  • Reduces starvation for long processes.

Round Robin (RR)

  • Each process gets a fixed time slice (quantum).
  • Cycles through all ready processes.
  • Fair and good for time-sharing, but performance depends on quantum size.

Priority Scheduling

  • Executes the highest-priority process next.
  • Can be preemptive or non-preemptive.
  • May lead to starvation if priorities aren't managed properly.

Multilevel Queue Scheduling

  • Divides processes into multiple queues by category.
  • Each queue has its own scheduling algorithm.
  • Good for organizing different types of processes, but complex to implement.

Multilevel Feedback Queue Scheduling

  • Allows processes to move between queues based on their behavior.
  • Adapts to process needs and reduces starvation.
  • Highly flexible but more complex and resource-intensive.
